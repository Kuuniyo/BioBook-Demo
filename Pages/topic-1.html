Key Concepts

Biological Systems can mean many things. In this case, we're referring to population systems.

Species: A species is a small group of different biological organisms that can reproduce viably and create offspring from their genetic makeup materials.

Population: A population is a whole group of a singular species (entities that can reproduce reliably) that inhabits an area.

Community: A community is a series of populations, whether of the same species or of different species, that live within one habitat.

Ecosystem: the big container for all of these subcategories together.

2. Structure Fits Function

In biology, the shape of something always relates to its job. Red blood cells are disc-shaped so they can squeeze through tiny capillaries and carry oxygen efficiently. Lung alveoli have thin, moist walls because gases need to diffuse quickly across them. This principle appears everywhere — remember it!

3. Systems Interact

No system works in isolation. When you run, your muscular system moves your legs, your circulatory system delivers extra oxygen, your respiratory system breathes faster, and your nervous system coordinates everything. The body is one giant, interconnected machine.

4. Feedback Loops Keep Things Stable

Systems monitor themselves and adjust. If your blood sugar rises, the pancreas releases insulin to bring it back down. If your body temperature drops, muscles shiver to generate heat. These are feedback mechanisms — you'll learn more about these in Section 5.

Key Definition
Biological System: A collection of organs, tissues, and cells that work together to perform one or more specific functions necessary for life.

Levels of Biological Organization

From atoms all the way to ecosystems

Biology organizes life into levels, from the smallest (atoms) to the largest (ecosystems). Each level is built from the one below it. Understanding these levels is critical for MEP — exam questions often ask you to place a structure at the correct level.
